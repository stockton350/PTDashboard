What Really Makes Us Happy?

By the Horizon Editorial Team  ·  Level B1–B2

For generations, people have pursued happiness as if it were a destination — a place to arrive at once they secure the right job, earn enough money, or buy the perfect home. Yet research in positive psychology tells a surprisingly different story.

Psychologist Sonja Lyubomirsky has spent decades studying what makes people genuinely content. Her findings suggest that the circumstances of our lives — where we live, how much we earn, even our relationship status — account for only around ten percent of our long-term satisfaction. Genetics explains roughly fifty percent. The remaining forty percent comes down to our own thoughts and deliberate behaviours.

So what behaviours actually boost your mood and lead to lasting well-being? The evidence points strongly in one direction: other people. Study after study confirms that maintaining meaningful relationships is the single most important factor in human happiness. A landmark 75-year study by Harvard University followed hundreds of people from adolescence into old age and reached a striking conclusion: the quality of our relationships — not wealth, fame, or professional achievement — is the greatest predictor of a happy life.

Experiences also appear to matter more than possessions. People who spend money on doing things — travelling, attending concerts, learning new skills — consistently report higher levels of satisfaction than those who spend the same amount on material goods. One reason, researchers suggest, is that experiences become part of our identity and our personal story in a way that objects never can.

Perhaps most interesting of all is what happens when people pursue happiness as a direct goal. Paradoxically, those who treat happiness as something to be actively chased often end up feeling less of it. Happiness, it seems, is more of a by-product than a destination — something that tends to emerge when we live with a genuine sense of purpose: working towards something that matters, contributing to others, and remaining curious about the world around us.

The lesson from the science of well-being is clear: resist the pull of instant gratification. Invest instead in your connections, your experiences, and your curiosity — and happiness will quietly follow.

"Happiness is not a destination. It is a by-product of a life well lived."

Critical Thinking 💡

Rank these activities from 1 (most likely to bring lasting happiness) to 5 (least likely). Then compare your ranking with a partner and explain your choices.

  • Buying something new and expensive
  • Spending an afternoon with close friends or family
  • Learning a new skill over several months
  • Watching your favourite TV series all evening
  • Doing something kind for a stranger, anonymously

Which activity did you rank first? Did your partner agree?

My World 🗣️

Think about a time when you felt genuinely happy. Tell your partner:

  • Where were you and who were you with?
  • What were you doing?
  • How long did that feeling last?

Try to use the present perfect and simple past in your answer.
